Read a book:

Reading a book is always a great choice especially when you find yourself with more time at home. Here are a few books we recommend.

China's Nonprofit Sector: Progress and Challenges (Asian Studies) edited by Dr. Chien-Chung Huang, Dr. Guosheng Deng, Dr. Zhenyao Wang, and Dr. Richard L. Edwards

Are you interested in learning about China’s nonprofit sector? This book is meant to inform Western audiences about the development of China’s civil society. This book describes the development of China’s nonprofit sector since 1995, including discussions on the rise of corporate responsibility and charitable foundations, grassroots organizations, and the microphilanthropy that arose after the Sichuan earthquake in 2008.

Little Green by Chun Yu

In this lyrical memoir, author Chun Yu recalls the Chinese Cultural Revolution from the perspective of a ten-year-old. Chun Yu was born in a small city in China in 1966, the year the Great Proletariat Cultural Revolution began. In spare poetry she remembers the first 10 years of her life.

The Making of Asian America by Erika Lee

A fascinating history of Asian Americans and their role in American life. Asian Americans have helped change the face of America and are now the fastest growing group in the United States. The Making of Asian America shows how generations of Asian immigrants and their American-born descendants have made and remade Asian American life, from sailors who came on the first trans-Pacific ships in the 1500s to the Japanese Americans incarcerated during World War II. Over the past 50 years, a new Asian America has emerged out of community activism and the arrival of new immigrants and refugees.

The Leavers by Lisa Ko

A complex portrait of a boy born to an undocumented Chinese immigrant who abandons him. He is adopted by a Caucasian couple and struggles to accept his new life and longs for his mother. This is Lisa Ko's debut novel, which was a finalist for the 2018 PEN/Hemingway Award, the 2017 Barnes and Noble Discover Great New Writers Award, and a finalist for the 2017 National Book Award for Fiction.

The Sympathizer by Viet Thanh Nguyen

The Sympathizer is a novel of betrayal, espionage, and the legacy of the Vietnam War, as Viet Thanh Nguyen juggles life between two worlds. The book gives the perspective of a confused, rebellious dreamer, examining one side of the war and its aftermath. It is a best-selling novel and recipient of the 2016 Pulitzer Prize for Fiction.

Song of the Exile by Kiana Davenport

This novel follows the life of the Meahuna family including one resilient man searching for his soul mate who was taken during the war. From the turbulent years of World War II through Hawaii's journey to statehood, it presents a cast of characters who rise up amidst chaos to embrace the love of their homeland.

Not Quite Not White: Losing and Finding Race in America by Sharmila Sen

Growing up in India, Sharmila Sen, a literary scholar and executive editor at Harvard Press, did not experience the concept of race. After moving to America at the age of 12 she discards her new "not quite" designation - not quite White, not quite Black, not quite Asian - and devotes her life to an attempt to blend into American whiteness. Sharmila discovers that “not quite White” is what makes her most American.
